A Stablecoin That
Pays Its Holders

How structural fairness changes what a stablecoin can be.

The question

What is a stablecoin actually for?

Most of us use them. Almost none of us ask what's happening with our dollar while it sits inside one.

This talk shows what's hidden inside today's stablecoins — and what changes when you fix it.

The market

Stablecoins are the rails of crypto.

$200B+

In circulating supply. Lending, trading, payments, savings — almost every crypto activity routes through one.

They're the most successful crypto product to date. Boring. Load-bearing. And economically one-sided.

The hidden trade

You hand Circle a dollar.
Circle keeps the yield.

When you hold USDC, Circle invests your dollar in U.S. Treasuries. At today's rates, that yield is roughly 4–5%.

You see none of it. The issuer keeps it.

USDT works the same way. So does PYUSD. So does almost every fully-collateralized stablecoin you've used.

By design, not by accident

This isn't a bug.
It's the business model.

Issue a stablecoin → scale it to a brand → capture the yield permanently. The holders take peg risk and counterparty risk. The issuer takes the upside.

It's legal. It's profitable. And it's structurally one-sided.

A different question

What if the yield went back?

Same dollar. Same collateral. Same redemption guarantee. Same auditability.

But the yield the reserves earn — flows back to the people holding the stablecoin. Not by issuer generosity. By the protocol's own math.

That's USD8.

The mechanism

The Cover Pool.

Traditional stablecoin

Reserves held by issuer. Yield held by issuer. Holders earn nothing.

USD8 Cover Pool

Reserves held in a transparent, auditable structure. Yield distributed to holders by mathematical rule, not by issuer discretion.

The constraint

By math, not by promise.

The issuer doesn't promise to share the yield.

The protocol enforces the distribution. There is no governance vote that can flip the deal. There is no executive decision that can claw it back.

The math is the constitutional court.

The fairness rule

Shapley value.

Nobel-prize-winning game theory, 1953.

Shapley answers a precise question: given a group that produces value together, what's each member's mathematically fair share?

It is the unique distribution rule that satisfies four basic fairness axioms simultaneously. Other rules break at least one.

In plain English

Three fairness properties.

Efficiency

Every dollar of yield is distributed. Nothing leaks to the issuer.

Symmetry

Holders who contributed equally receive equal shares. No favoritism.

Null player

Holders who contributed nothing receive nothing. No free riders.

If you held more USD8 longer, you earned more. Math, not policy.

The proof

USD8's distribution is Brevis-verifiable: cryptographic proofs anyone can check, without trusting the issuer's books.

You don't have to take our word for it. You don't have to trust the audit firm. The math is publicly checkable, every distribution, every block.

Trust the verification, not the verifier.

For users

Hold it. Earn from it. That's it.

No staking. No locking. No yield farming. No active management.

You hold USD8. The protocol's earnings flow to you proportionally. The longer and more you hold, the more you earn — by structural rule.

It works the same way the dollar in your pocket would, if your dollar earned T-bill yield.

For developers

A substrate, not a brand.

USD8 is a stablecoin other protocols can build on, with structural fairness already baked in.

If your protocol pays users in USD8, your users get a second layer of yield from USD8 itself — without you having to engineer it.

The math composes.
