# LinkedIn Post 11 — Scale of One

---

9,090 tests. 290 contracts. Zero employees.

Everyone talks about "AI-augmented development." Here's what it actually looks like:

You sit down at 8am. By midnight, you've written a batch auction mechanism, a game-theoretic reward system, cross-chain messaging, a Kalman filter oracle, and 45 new tests — all verified, all passing, all committed.

No standup. No Jira ticket. No waiting on a PR review from someone who doesn't understand the architecture because they joined last month.

This isn't about replacing developers. It's about what happens when one mind holds the entire system in their head and has a copilot that can keep up.

A funded team of 15 would've spent six months on what we built in weeks. Not because they're worse engineers — because coordination costs scale quadratically with team size. Brooks's Law isn't a suggestion. It's physics.

The myth of software development is that big things require big teams. The reality is that big teams are a tax on clarity. Every additional person is another translation layer between the idea and the code.

I'm not saying solo is always better. I'm saying the threshold for what one person can build just moved by an order of magnitude. And most of the industry hasn't caught up to that yet.

9,090 tests don't lie. The code works or it doesn't.

What's the biggest thing you've built alone?
