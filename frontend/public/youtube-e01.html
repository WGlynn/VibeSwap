VibeSwap // EP 01

Why does
this exist?

A teaching series on VibeSwap, Augmented Mechanism Design, and Cooperative Capitalism.
Welcome to the first episode of the VibeSwap series. We're going to spend the next several episodes walking through what VibeSwap is, why it exists, and how it works under the hood. But before any of the mechanics, we need to start with the question that motivated the whole project: why does this need to exist at all? That's what this episode is for.
crypto.state(2014..2025)→user_loss

Crypto has been bleeding users for a decade.

Bridge hacks. Rug pulls. Nine-figure exploits that dominate the news cycle for a week and disappear into the next one.

You know the pattern. So do I.

The numbness is the part that hurts. The fact that we've come to expect this. The fact that another exploit barely registers anymore.

Bridge hacks, rug pulls, nine-figure exploits. We've all watched it for a decade. And honestly the worst part isn't the loss itself — it's the numbness. We've stopped expecting better. That's the problem this series is pushing back on, starting with the question of why VibeSwap exists at all.
decentralization.tradeoff()→enforcement_lost

We freed crypto from centralized power. We also freed the bad actors.

The decentralization movement stripped out gatekeepers. Good. It also stripped out the enforcement layer that punishes wrongdoing.

No courts. No recovery systems. No cross-border cooperation against bad actors. So we live in an industry where hackers and malicious founders steal in broad daylight with almost no consequence.

Gresham's Law plays out in real time. Good actors get driven out. Bad actors stay. The space slowly hollows.

Decentralization was the right call — it freed us from gatekeepers, from KYC walls, from being told who's allowed to participate. But it also dissolved the enforcement layer. There are no courts in DeFi. No recovery systems. No cross-border cooperation against the people exploiting users. And what happens in any system with no consequences for bad behavior? Gresham's Law: good actors get driven out, bad actors stay. The space hollows. That's where we are.
crypto.attempted_fix("code is law")→false

Crypto tried to fix this once. It didn't work.

"Code is Law" was the original thesis: smart contract immutability would prevent misconduct. No need for courts — the contract IS the contract.

It didn't survive contact with reality. Smart contracts have bugs. Upgrades are practically necessary. Immutability turned out to be a constraint we couldn't actually keep.

We didn't replace the solution. We just stopped trying.

We did try once. "Code is Law" was the early thesis — smart contract immutability would prevent misconduct, you wouldn't need courts because the contract itself was the rule. But that ran into reality fast. Smart contracts have bugs. Upgrades are necessary. Immutability turned out to be aspirational. And after Code-is-Law failed, we didn't come up with a replacement. We just stopped trying.

— what VibeSwap is, in one sentence
So here's VibeSwap in one line. It's a decentralized exchange built so that dishonesty isn't just discouraged or monitored — it's structurally unprofitable. Designed from the ground up around that principle, not patched on top of an extractive base. The rest of this series is going to unpack what that actually means in mechanism, in math, and in code. But the one-line version is that.
methodology.AMD()→augment_dont_replace

Augmented Mechanism Design.

// Don't
Replace markets with central control.
Markets do information aggregation better than committees do. Replacing them loses too much.
// Do
Augment them with math-enforced rules.
Add orthogonal protective layers — cryptography, fairness invariants, automated penalties — that close failure modes without disabling what already works.
// the test
Pull the augmentation out. If the protocol still works — just dumber — it's augmentation. If it collapses, it's replacement.
Underneath VibeSwap is a methodology I call Augmented Mechanism Design. The idea is — don't replace markets with central control. Markets are actually really good at information aggregation, much better than any committee or algorithm. Replacing them loses too much. Instead, augment them — add orthogonal protective layers, math-enforced rules, that close the failure modes without disabling the parts that already work. The test for whether something is real AMD or fake AMD: pull the new piece out. If the protocol still works, just with worse properties, it's augmentation. If pulling it collapses the whole thing, it's replacement.
philosophy.coop_capitalism()→layer_separation

Cooperative Capitalism.

// Cooperative layer (mutualize)
Risk.
Insurance pools, treasury reserves, loss protection — these belong on the cooperative layer, mutualized across everyone in the system.
// Competitive layer (compete)
Value.
Arbitrage, liquidity provision, market discovery — these stay open to anyone who can create value. Free competition where it produces good outcomes.
// the principle
Cooperation and competition aren't opposites. They operate on different layers — and the design of a system determines which layer each one lives on.
And the philosophy that AMD serves is what I call Cooperative Capitalism. The principle is layer separation. Mutualize the risk layer — insurance, treasury, loss protection — those should be cooperative, shared across everyone. Compete on the value layer — arbitrage, liquidity provision, market discovery — those stay free and open. Cooperation and competition aren't opposites. They live on different layers, and the design of the system determines which layer each one operates on. DeFi historically chose freedom and got extraction. TradFi chose protection and got gatekeeping. Cooperative Capitalism is the third path.
