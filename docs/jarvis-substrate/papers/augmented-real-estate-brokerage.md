# Augmented Real Estate Brokerage

The U.S. residential real estate brokerage substrate operated as a structural cartel for decades — 6% commissions, split between buyer's agent and seller's agent, with the seller paying both sides. The structure was challenged in the Burnett v. NAR antitrust case (settled 2024 for $418M plus structural changes). The settlement requires changes that may or may not produce material market structure change. The substrate is mid-transition.

The current alternatives are continued traditional brokerage (still dominant despite changes), discount brokerage (limited adoption), iBuyer models (Zillow Offers, Opendoor — mixed success), and direct FSBO (For Sale By Owner — limited fraction of transactions). Each addresses parts of the failure mode.

The right response is augmentation: preserve competitive brokerage markets where agents compete on actual service quality, mutualize the listing infrastructure and verification layer where collective infrastructure improves market function, and add specific protective extensions that close the commission-cartel, agent-incentive-misalignment, and information-asymmetry failure modes.

---

## The pure mechanism

Sellers list properties, typically through agents listed in regional Multiple Listing Services (MLS). Buyers' agents search MLS and show properties to clients. Sellers pay commission on sale (historically 6%, split between buyer and seller agents). Agents are licensed by states; brokerages employ them; the National Association of Realtors maintains industry standards.

The Burnett settlement requires changes including buyer agents negotiating commissions directly with buyers (rather than through seller-paid structure), commission disclosure changes, and various structural modifications. Implementation effects are still emerging.

---

## Failure modes

**Commission cartel structure.** Seller-pays-both-sides 6% commission persisted for decades despite the international comparison showing that other developed countries operate at 1-3% commissions for similar service. The structure was maintained through MLS rules, NAR practices, and agent training that effectively enforced the commission norm.

**Agent-incentive misalignment.** Buyers' agents' commission scales with purchase price, incentivizing higher prices. Sellers' agents' commission scales with sale price but the marginal commission on incremental price is small relative to the agent's interest in transaction completion — incentivizing quick sale at acceptable price rather than maximum price.

**Information asymmetry.** Agents have substantial information advantage over typical buyers and sellers (MLS access, market knowledge, transaction experience). The asymmetry has been compressed somewhat by Zillow, Redfin, and similar platforms but remains significant.

**Dual agency conflicts.** When the same agent or brokerage represents both buyer and seller, the fiduciary duty conflicts. Some states prohibit dual agency; others permit with disclosure. The structural pattern produces predictable failures regardless of formal rules.

**Discount-brokerage failure to scale.** Discount brokerage models (Redfin, others) have captured small market share despite offering substantial savings. The MLS structure and agent-network effects have protected traditional brokerage even after settlement-driven changes.

**Foreign-buyer money-laundering vulnerability.** Real estate transactions provide structural opportunity for money laundering. Title insurance and escrow processes provide some screening but the substrate has been substantially exploited. Recent FinCEN rules address parts of this for cash transactions in specific markets.

---

## Layer mapping

**Mutualize the listing infrastructure and verification layer.** MLS access, property data, transaction verification, and quality measurement are collective goods. The current architecture has MLS access controlled by brokerages with structural exclusion of competitors; the augmented architecture treats listing infrastructure as common substrate.

**Compete on agent service quality and brokerage differentiation.** Agents and brokerages should fight freely on actually serving clients well. The competitive layer is where genuine service differentiation matters.

The current architecture has these reversed. Listing infrastructure is brokerage-controlled. Service competition is constrained by commission-cartel structure. The augmented architecture mutualizes infrastructure; opens competition.

---

## Augmentations

**Open MLS access with structural anti-discrimination.** MLS data accessible to any licensed agent, FSBO seller, or qualified buyer — not just NAR-affiliated brokerage agents. The structural exclusion that protected commission cartel weakens.

**Cryptographic transaction provenance.** Real estate transactions cryptographically tracked from listing through closing. Foreign-buyer money-laundering vulnerability gets structurally compressed. Title disputes face structural evidence.

**Anti-dual-agency structural rules.** Dual agency prohibited or significantly constrained across jurisdictions. The structural conflict that produces predictable failures gets compressed.

**Buyer-agent commission negotiation infrastructure.** Standardized buyer-agent agreements with structural commission negotiation, post-Burnett. Buyers gain real ability to negotiate agent compensation rather than accepting market-default rates.

**Discount-brokerage structural support.** New brokerage models gain structural support for MLS access and listing infrastructure. The network-effect protection of traditional brokerage compresses.

**Agent-quality measurement.** Agent performance measured structurally (transaction outcomes, client satisfaction, market knowledge demonstration). Buyers and sellers can compare agents on actual performance.

**Title and escrow modernization.** Title verification and escrow processes use cryptographic provenance, reducing transaction friction and time. The current pattern of title issues being substantial transaction risk gets compressed.

---

## Implementation reality

Burnett settlement is producing changes; implementation effects are still emerging. State licensing structures vary. NAR has substantial political influence. The augmentation pattern integrates with post-settlement evolution.

---

## What changes

If implemented at scale:

First, commission rates compress toward international norms. The structural cartel that maintained 6% commissions weakens; competition produces lower rates for comparable service.

Second, agent-client alignment improves. Anti-dual-agency rules reduce conflict; transparency in commission negotiation reduces information asymmetry.

Third, market structure becomes more competitive. Discount brokerage and alternative models gain structural support; the traditional-brokerage dominance compresses.

The downstream effect is real estate transactions where buyers and sellers retain more value rather than transferring 6% to agents per transaction.

The same methodology that protected fair distribution would protect home buyers and sellers from cartel-driven extraction. The substrate is mid-transition. The methodology is the same.
