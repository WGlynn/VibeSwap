# Augmented Antitrust Enforcement

The antitrust substrate has been failing for decades through a specific failure mode: enforcement asymmetric to where market power actually concentrates. Tech platforms (Google search, Apple App Store, Amazon marketplace, Meta social) operate with substantial market power that's gone largely unaddressed; major mergers proceed with limited scrutiny; the consumer-welfare standard that has dominated antitrust thinking since the 1970s under-recognizes harms that don't show up directly in consumer prices.

The current alternatives are continued narrow consumer-welfare enforcement (status quo), revival of structural antitrust (advocated by some recent FTC and DOJ leadership but slow to materialize), and ex-ante regulation (EU Digital Markets Act, similar emerging frameworks). Each addresses parts of the failure mode.

The right response is augmentation: preserve competitive markets where competition produces consumer benefit, mutualize the verification and detection layer where collective infrastructure improves enforcement, and add specific protective extensions that close the platform-monopoly, merger-cascade, and labor-market-power failure modes.

---

## The pure mechanism

Antitrust law (Sherman Act, Clayton Act, FTC Act in U.S.; equivalent in other jurisdictions) prohibits anticompetitive conduct, monopolization, and mergers that substantially lessen competition. Enforcement is administrative (FTC, DOJ Antitrust Division) and private (treble-damages litigation by injured parties). Courts develop the doctrine through specific cases.

Since the 1970s Chicago School influence, U.S. antitrust enforcement has narrowed to consumer-welfare-as-price effects. Mergers that don't visibly raise consumer prices generally proceed. Practices that don't visibly raise consumer prices generally aren't prosecuted. The narrow doctrine has produced predictably narrow enforcement.

---

## Failure modes

**Platform monopolies underenforced.** Google search market share exceeds 90%; Apple App Store charges 30% on iOS app revenue; Amazon dominates third-party marketplace; Meta dominates several social-media categories. Each operates with substantial market power that produces structural rent extraction. Enforcement has been slow and uneven; the underlying market structure persists.

**Merger cascade underscrutinized.** Industries have consolidated dramatically over decades — banking, airlines, telecom, healthcare, agriculture. The cumulative consolidation reduces competition across many substrates. Individual mergers within consolidating industries often proceed because each individual merger doesn't visibly cross consumer-welfare thresholds.

**Labor-market monopsony.** Antitrust law has historically focused on consumer-side market power. Labor-market monopsony (employer concentration giving employers wage-suppression power) has been substantially under-enforced. Specific examples (poultry processing in some regions, healthcare in some markets, tech-industry no-poach agreements) have been addressed but the structural pattern persists.

**Vertical integration extraction.** Companies vertically integrating to exclude competitors from inputs or distribution face limited scrutiny under current doctrine. The pattern produces market structures where competitors face structural disadvantage independent of their actual capability.

**Algorithmic collusion.** Pricing algorithms can produce coordinated outcomes that resemble collusion without explicit communication between competitors. The current doctrine struggles with algorithmic patterns that don't fit traditional collusion categories.

**Common ownership (institutional investors).** Major institutional investors (BlackRock, Vanguard, State Street) hold substantial stakes in nominally competing companies in concentrated industries. The common ownership may reduce competitive intensity even without active coordination. The doctrine hasn't yet integrated this dynamic.

---

## Layer mapping

**Mutualize the detection and verification layer.** Market concentration measurement, anticompetitive-conduct pattern detection, and merger impact assessment are collective goods. Every market participant benefits when competition is structurally protected.

**Compete on actual product and service quality.** Companies should fight freely on what they offer to consumers. Antitrust shouldn't constrain genuine competition; it should constrain the extraction that monopoly power enables.

The current architecture has detection limited (enforcement agencies under-resourced relative to economy scale). The augmented architecture mutualizes detection through structural pattern monitoring; preserves competition on product quality.

---

## Augmentations

**Cryptographic market-concentration tracking.** Industry concentration metrics get cryptographically tracked across sectors and jurisdictions. Mergers proposed in already-concentrated sectors face structural higher scrutiny. The current pattern of merger-by-merger evaluation that misses cumulative effects gets compressed.

**Algorithmic-collusion detection infrastructure.** Pricing patterns analyzed structurally for algorithmic-collusion signatures. Markets where pricing converges in patterns inconsistent with independent decision-making face structural investigation. The current doctrine gap that lets algorithmic collusion proceed gets addressed.

**Labor-market monopsony enforcement parity.** Antitrust enforcement allocates structural attention to labor-market monopsony alongside consumer-market monopoly. Wage suppression from employer concentration gets enforced as structural antitrust matter rather than as ad-hoc labor issue.

**Common-ownership transparency.** Institutional-investor positions in concentrated industries get structural disclosure. Common ownership patterns inconsistent with competitive markets face structural investigation.

**Anti-vertical-integration structural rules.** Vertical integration into adjacent markets faces structural scrutiny when the parent has market power in the original market. The platform-monopoly extension into adjacent markets gets structural friction.

**Ex-ante platform regulation (DMA-style).** Designated gatekeeper platforms face structural rules — interoperability requirements, anti-self-preferencing, data portability — without requiring case-by-case enforcement. The EU DMA provides a working precedent; the augmentation extends globally.

**Private-action structural support.** Treble-damage private antitrust litigation gets structural support for plaintiffs facing well-resourced defendants. The asymmetric resources that have made private antitrust enforcement weak get compressed through structural mechanisms.

---

## Implementation reality

Antitrust reform has been politically active recently — FTC chair Lina Khan, DOJ Antitrust under Jonathan Kanter, similar appointees in EU. The challenge is doctrinal — courts have continued applying narrow consumer-welfare standard despite enforcement-agency push for broader scrutiny.

Staging path is venue-by-venue. EU has been most receptive to ex-ante platform regulation. U.S. has been more case-by-case. Specific industries (tech platforms, healthcare consolidation, specific merger reviews) have been venues for doctrinal experimentation.

---

## What changes

If implemented at scale:

First, platform monopolies face structural friction. Self-preferencing, anti-competitive bundling, and similar practices get structurally constrained. Competitive entry into platform-dominated markets becomes feasible.

Second, merger cascade slows. Consolidation in already-concentrated industries faces structural higher scrutiny. The cumulative concentration trend that has marked recent decades reverses.

Third, labor-market power gets enforced. Wage suppression from employer concentration faces structural enforcement matching consumer-market enforcement. Worker bargaining power in concentrated labor markets improves.

The downstream effect is competitive markets that actually compete, with antitrust enforcement matched to where market power actually concentrates.

The same methodology that protected fair distribution would protect market participants from extractive market power. The substrate is doctrinally constrained. The methodology is the same.

---

*Antitrust enforcement has narrowed while market concentration has widened. The augmentation re-aligns enforcement with where the failure modes actually occur.*
