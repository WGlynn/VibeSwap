## Abstract

This paper presents a reward distribution system for decentralized networks based on the Shapley value from cooperative game theory. The system treats each value-creating event as an independent cooperative game, computes each participant's marginal contribution across all possible coalition orderings, and distributes rewards in exact proportion to actual contribution. The design enforces four mathematical guarantees: efficiency (all value is distributed), symmetry (equal contributors receive equal rewards), linearity (rewards are additive across games), and the null player property (non-contributors receive nothing). We demonstrate that this system is anti-MLM by construction -- rewards are bounded by real value creation and cannot compound beyond the revenue generated by each event. We show that the system recognizes enabling contributions (participants whose early activity made later value creation possible) without overpaying them. We prove that the resulting incentive structure makes cooperation the dominant strategy, early participation valuable without being extractive, late participation viable without penalty, and growth cooperative rather than competitive. The system is implemented in VibeSwap as `ShapleyDistributor.sol` and governs LP reward distribution, referral rewards, and cross-DAO value sharing.

> "Distribute rewards in proportion to actual contribution, including synergistic and enabling effects, without minting value from nothing."

## 1. Introduction

Every decentralized network faces the same fundamental question: how should value be distributed among participants?

The question seems simple. The answers are not. Over the past decade, the crypto ecosystem has experimented with dozens of distribution mechanisms: flat-rate airdrops, liquidity mining, yield farming, referral bonuses, staking rewards, and multi-level incentive structures. Each has produced perverse outcomes.

Airdrops reward addresses, not contributions. Liquidity mining rewards capital, not participation. Yield farming rewards mercenary capital that leaves the moment incentives decline. Referral bonuses reward the referrer as if they created all the value the referral generates. Multi-level systems create compounding structures that eventually exceed the real value created by the network.

The common flaw in all these systems is the same: they distribute rewards based on *proxies* for contribution (capital deposited, referrals made, tokens staked) rather than on *actual marginal contribution* to the cooperative enterprise.

This paper proposes a different approach. Drawing on Shapley value theory from cooperative game theory, we design a reward system that distributes value in exact proportion to each participant's actual contribution, including indirect and enabling contributions, without minting value from nothing and without permitting extraction beyond what was collectively created.

## 2. Why Traditional Reward Systems Fail

### 2.1 Overpaying the Early and the Loud

Traditional reward systems tend to concentrate value in the hands of early participants and high-visibility contributors, regardless of whether their contribution justified the reward.

First-mover advantages in flat referral systems are extreme: the first participant to refer others captures a disproportionate share of the network's growth, even if their actual contribution to that growth was minimal. Similarly, governance token distributions tend to reward those who are most visible in governance discussions, not those whose proposals create the most value.

### 2.2 Rigid Hierarchies Become Extractive

Any system with fixed roles (admin, moderator, curator, oracle) tends toward extraction over time. The role-holder's power increases as the network grows, but their marginal contribution often decreases. A moderator who was essential when the community had 100 members may be redundant when it has 100,000, but the moderator's rewards continue because the role persists.

### 2.3 Flat Referrals Assume Total Causation

A flat referral bonus assumes that the referrer created ALL the value of the referral. If Alice refers Bob, and Bob generates $100 in trading fees, a flat 10% referral bonus pays Alice $10. But did Alice create $10 in value? Perhaps she simply mentioned the protocol in passing and Bob would have found it anyway. Perhaps the protocol's UX, liquidity, and fee structure -- created by dozens of other contributors -- were the actual reasons Bob stayed and traded.

Flat referral systems cannot distinguish between these scenarios. They assume the referrer is the sole cause of the referral's value. This assumption is almost always wrong.

### 2.4 Multi-Level Compounding Exceeds Real Value

Multi-level marketing (MLM) structures compound rewards across levels:

```
Level 0: Alice refers Bob        → Alice gets 10% of Bob's fees
Level 1: Bob refers Carol        → Alice gets 5% of Carol's fees
Level 2: Carol refers Dave       → Alice gets 2.5% of Dave's fees
Level 3: Dave refers Eve         → Alice gets 1.25% of Eve's fees
...
```

The problem is that these percentages compound across the entire network. If the network grows large enough, the sum of all referral obligations can exceed the total value created by the network. This is the defining characteristic of an unsustainable pyramid structure: obligations grow faster than revenue.

## 3. The Glove Game

The Shapley value is best understood through a simple example.

Three players: Alice has one left glove, Bob has one right glove, Carol has nothing.

| Coalition | Value | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| {} | $0 | No players, no value |
| {Alice} | $0 | One left glove has no value alone |
| {Bob} | $0 | One right glove has no value alone |
| {Carol} | $0 | Nothing has no value |
| {Alice, Bob} | $1 | A pair of gloves is worth $1 |
| {Alice, Carol} | $0 | A left glove plus nothing = $0 |
| {Bob, Carol} | $0 | A right glove plus nothing = $0 |
| {Alice, Bob, Carol} | $1 | A pair of gloves is still worth $1 |

The question: how should the $1 be divided?

**Intuitive answer**: Alice and Bob each get $0.50. Carol gets nothing. Neither Alice nor Bob "deserves" the full dollar, because neither can create value alone. The value exists *only* because of cooperation. Carol receives nothing because she contributes nothing.

**Formal answer**: The Shapley value computation considers every possible ordering of players joining the coalition and computes each player's marginal contribution in each ordering:

| Ordering | Alice's marginal | Bob's marginal | Carol's marginal |
|---|---|---|---|
| A, B, C | 0 (A alone = $0) | 1 (A+B = $1) | 0 (A+B+C = $1) |
| A, C, B | 0 | 1 | 0 |
| B, A, C | 1 (A completes the pair) | 0 (B alone = $0) | 0 |
| B, C, A | 1 | 0 | 0 |
| C, A, B | 0 | 1 | 0 |
| C, B, A | 1 | 0 | 0 |

**Average marginal contribution**:
- Alice: (0 + 0 + 1 + 1 + 0 + 1) / 6 = **$0.50**
- Bob: (1 + 1 + 0 + 0 + 1 + 0) / 6 = **$0.50**
- Carol: (0 + 0 + 0 + 0 + 0 + 0) / 6 = **$0.00**

This is the unique fair allocation. It is not negotiable, not subjective, not political. It is mathematically determined by the structure of the game.

## 4. Shapley Value: Formal Definition

For a cooperative game (N, v) where N is the set of players and v is the characteristic function mapping each coalition S to its value v(S), the Shapley value for player i is:

```
φ_i(v) = Σ_{S ⊆ N\{i}}  [ |S|! (|N| - |S| - 1)! / |N|! ]  ×  [ v(S ∪ {i}) - v(S) ]
```

In words: for every possible coalition S that does not include player i, compute player i's marginal contribution to that coalition [v(S ∪ {i}) - v(S)], weight it by the probability that i joins at exactly that point in a random ordering, and sum over all coalitions.

The weighting factor `|S|! (|N| - |S| - 1)! / |N|!` ensures that each ordering of players is equally likely, making the result independent of any particular ordering or sequence of events.

## 5. The Four Axioms

The Shapley value is the *unique* distribution function satisfying four axioms simultaneously:

### 5.1 Efficiency

All value is distributed. Nothing is wasted. Nothing is held back.

```
Σ_{i ∈ N} φ_i(v) = v(N)
```

The sum of all players' Shapley values equals the total value created by the grand coalition. There is no surplus (unclaimed value) and no deficit (value distributed that was not created).

### 5.2 Symmetry

Equal contributors receive equal rewards. If players i and j make the same marginal contribution to every coalition, they receive the same Shapley value.

```
If v(S ∪ {i}) = v(S ∪ {j}) for all S ⊆ N\{i,j}, then φ_i(v) = φ_j(v)
```

This prevents discrimination based on anything other than contribution. Identity, seniority, reputation, or social status do not affect the distribution unless they affect the actual marginal contribution.

### 5.3 Linearity (Additivity)

If a player participates in two independent games, their total reward is the sum of their rewards in each game. There is no penalty for diversification and no bonus for concentration.

```
φ_i(v + w) = φ_i(v) + φ_i(w)
```

This property is what makes event-based games (Section 7) work: each event is a separate game, and a participant's total reward across all events is the sum of their rewards in each event.

### 5.4 Null Player

A player who contributes nothing to any coalition receives nothing.

```
If v(S ∪ {i}) = v(S) for all S ⊆ N\{i}, then φ_i(v) = 0
```

This is the most important axiom for preventing extraction. A participant who adds no marginal value to any coalition -- who could be removed without reducing the value of any coalition they belong to -- receives exactly zero. There are no participation trophies, no seniority bonuses, and no rewards for merely existing.

## 6. Design Decisions

The theoretical Shapley framework must be adapted for practical use in decentralized networks. The following design decisions bridge theory and implementation.

### 6.1 Summary of Key Decisions

| Decision | Choice | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **Game scope** | Event-based (not network-wide) | Keeps coalitions small, computation feasible, incentives local |
| **Value source** | Realized revenue only | Prevents inflation, bounds rewards by reality |
| **Multiplier scope** | Coalition-wide (not individual) | Preserves symmetry, prevents gaming |
| **Quality weights** | Modify marginal contribution, don't create value | Weights adjust distribution, don't generate rewards |
| **Update frequency** | Epoch-based | Balances accuracy with computational cost |
| **Compounding** | Limited to realized events | Prevents MLM dynamics |

## 7. Event-Based Games

The most critical design decision is that the system does NOT compute one massive Shapley game for the entire network. Instead, each value-creating event is treated as an independent cooperative game.

### 7.1 Definition of an Event

An event is any discrete, observable occurrence that generates value for the network:

| Event Type | Coalition | Value Created |
|---|---|---|
| Referral conversion | Referrer(s) + new user | Fees from new user's first trade(s) |
| Liquidity provision | LP + traders using the pool | Trading fees generated by the pool |
| Governance proposal | Proposer + voters + implementers | Value of the implemented change |
| Oracle attestation | Oracle operator + consumers | Fees paid for price data |
| Cross-chain settlement | Bridge operator + sender + receiver | Bridge fees (if any) |

### 7.2 Why Event-Based

**Computational feasibility**: Shapley value computation is O(2^n) in the number of players. A network-wide game with millions of participants is intractable. Event-based games have 2-10 participants each, making exact computation trivial.

**Incentive locality**: Participants can see exactly which events their rewards came from and exactly how their marginal contribution was computed. This transparency creates trust in the system.

**Temporal isolation**: Each event is self-contained. A participant's reward from Event A is not affected by anything that happens in Event B. This prevents cascading effects and simplifies auditing.

### 7.3 Example: Referral Chain

Alice refers Bob. Bob refers Carol. Carol makes a trade that generates $100 in fees.

The event is Carol's trade. The coalition is {Alice, Bob, Carol}. The characteristic function is:

```
v({}) = $0
v({Alice}) = $0          (Alice alone generates no fees)
v({Bob}) = $0             (Bob alone generates no fees)
v({Carol}) = $100         (Carol made the trade)
v({Alice, Bob}) = $0      (Neither generated fees without Carol)
v({Alice, Carol}) = $100  (Alice referred the chain, Carol traded)
v({Bob, Carol}) = $100    (Bob directly referred Carol, Carol traded)
v({Alice, Bob, Carol}) = $100

φ(Alice) = $16.67
φ(Bob) = $16.67
φ(Carol) = $66.67
```

Carol receives the largest share because she created the most marginal value (she generated the fees). Alice and Bob receive equal shares because they made symmetric enabling contributions (each was part of the referral chain). Nobody receives more than the $100 that was actually created.

## 8. No Value, No Rewards

### 8.1 The Sustainability Constraint

> "Rewards cannot exceed revenue."

If an event generates no value, the Shapley computation produces zero rewards for all participants. This is not a limitation -- it is the core sustainability mechanism.

```
If v(N) = 0, then φ_i(v) = 0 for all i ∈ N
```

This constraint prevents:

- **Inflation**: Tokens are not minted to pay rewards. Rewards are drawn from realized revenue.
- **Speculative compounding**: Participants cannot earn rewards on expected future value. Only realized value is distributed.
- **Phantom value**: A referral that generates no economic activity produces no reward, regardless of how many people were referred.

### 8.2 Comparison with Traditional Systems

| System | Value Source | Inflation Risk |
|---|---|---|
| **Liquidity mining** | Newly minted tokens | High (dilutes existing holders) |
| **Flat referral bonus** | Protocol treasury or minting | Medium (bounded per event but unbounded in aggregate) |
| **MLM** | Combination of new user deposits and minting | Extreme (obligations compound faster than revenue) |
| **Shapley** | Realized event revenue only | Zero (rewards = revenue, by efficiency axiom) |

## 9. Local vs. Global Value

### 9.1 Local Rewards

Each event produces a *local reward* -- the value created by that specific event. This is the base Shapley distribution.

### 9.2 Global Multipliers

Optionally, a *global multiplier* can scale local rewards to reflect network-wide effects:

```
adjusted_reward_i = φ_i(v) × global_multiplier
```

The global multiplier might reflect:

- **Network growth rate**: Higher during expansion phases, encouraging early participation
- **Diversity factor**: Higher for contributions in underserved areas (new markets, new asset pairs)
- **Duration factor**: Higher for sustained participation over multiple epochs

### 9.3 The Critical Design Choice

**Multipliers apply to the ENTIRE COALITION, not to individuals.**

This preserves the symmetry axiom. If Alice and Bob make equal contributions and the global multiplier is 1.5x, both receive 1.5x their base Shapley value. Alice cannot increase her multiplier without also increasing Bob's. This eliminates an entire class of gaming strategies: a participant cannot manipulate their personal multiplier to extract more than their fair share.

```
// CORRECT: coalition-wide multiplier
adjusted_φ(Alice) = φ(Alice) × M
adjusted_φ(Bob) = φ(Bob) × M
// Symmetry preserved: if φ(Alice) = φ(Bob), then adjusted values are equal

// INCORRECT: individual multiplier (this would break symmetry)
adjusted_φ(Alice) = φ(Alice) × M_Alice
adjusted_φ(Bob) = φ(Bob) × M_Bob
// Symmetry broken: even if φ(Alice) = φ(Bob), adjusted values differ
```

## 10. Quality-Weighted Contributions

### 10.1 Quality Dimensions

Each participant has a quality weight computed from four dimensions:

| Dimension | What It Measures | How It Is Computed |
|---|---|---|
| **Activity** | Frequency and consistency of participation | Transaction count, voting participation, proposal submissions per epoch |
| **Economic contribution** | Volume generated, liquidity provided, fees paid | On-chain metrics aggregated per epoch |
| **Social trust** | Reputation in the community | Vouches received, dispute outcomes, DAG position |
| **ELO-style pairwise** | Relative standing among peers | Pairwise comparisons based on outcome quality |

### 10.2 How Weights Affect Distribution

Quality weights *modify* the characteristic function used in Shapley computation. A high-quality participant's marginal contribution is weighted upward, reflecting the fact that their participation creates more value per unit of effort.

```
v_weighted(S ∪ {i}) - v_weighted(S) = [v(S ∪ {i}) - v(S)] × quality_weight(i) / avg_quality(S ∪ {i})
```

**Critical constraint**: Quality weights do NOT create value on their own. A participant with the highest possible quality weight who contributes nothing to any coalition still receives zero (null player axiom). Weights adjust the distribution of existing value; they do not generate new value.

### 10.3 Epoch-Based Updates

Quality weights are updated at epoch boundaries (not continuously). This:

- Reduces computation cost (one update per epoch, not per event)
- Prevents gaming through rapid quality manipulation
- Provides stability (participants know their weight for the entire epoch)
- Enables auditing (weights are fixed and verifiable for each epoch)

## 11. Anti-MLM by Construction

### 11.1 Why MLM Systems Fail

Multi-level marketing structures fail because they create obligations that compound faster than the value they generate:

```
Level 0: Alice earns 10% of all downline revenue
Level 1: Bob earns 10% of his downline revenue
Level 2: Carol earns 10% of her downline revenue
...

Total obligations = 10% + 10% of 90% + 10% of 81% + ...
                  = Σ 0.1 × 0.9^n
                  → Converges but approaches 100% of all revenue
```

As the network grows, the cumulative obligations approach or exceed 100% of revenue. The system must either inflate its currency, attract new money faster than obligations grow (Ponzi dynamics), or collapse.

### 11.2 Why Shapley Cannot Be MLM

The Shapley system is structurally incapable of MLM dynamics because of the efficiency axiom:

```
Σ_{i ∈ N} φ_i(v) = v(N)
```

The total distributed across all participants equals *exactly* the value created. Not more. Not less. There is no compounding across levels because there are no levels. Each event is a flat cooperative game where everyone's marginal contribution is computed independently.

Moreover:

- **No value = no rewards** (Section 8). Obligations cannot exceed revenue because rewards ARE revenue.
- **Event isolation** (Section 7). Carol's event does not create obligations for Alice's event. There is no cascade.
- **Null player axiom** (Section 5.4). Participants who do not contribute to an event receive nothing from it, regardless of their position in any referral chain.
- **Symmetry axiom** (Section 5.2). There is no structural advantage to being "above" someone in a hierarchy because there is no hierarchy.

> "The system naturally converges toward fair outcomes because unfair distributions are structurally impossible."

### 11.3 Comparison Table

| Property | MLM | Flat Referral | Shapley |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Reward source** | Downline revenue + minting | Fixed bonus per referral | Realized event value only |
| **Compounding** | Unlimited across levels | None (but per-event) | None (event-isolated) |
| **Value bound** | Obligations can exceed revenue | Per-event bounded, aggregate unbounded | Mathematically bounded: rewards = value |
| **Hierarchy** | Essential (levels define rewards) | Minimal (referrer/referee) | None (flat coalition) |
| **Late joiner penalty** | Severe (bottom of pyramid) | None | None (contribution-based) |
| **Sustainability** | Structurally unsustainable | Depends on bonus rate | Provably sustainable (efficiency axiom) |

## 12. Enabling Contributions

### 12.1 The Problem

Traditional reward systems struggle with a specific class of contributions: *enabling* contributions that do not directly create value but make it possible for others to do so.

Consider: Alice builds a liquidity pool. Bob provides liquidity. Carol trades against the pool, generating $100 in fees. Who created the value?

Carol made the trade. But without Bob's liquidity, there would be nothing to trade against. And without Alice's pool, Bob could not have provided liquidity. Alice's contribution was *enabling* -- she created the infrastructure that made Bob's and Carol's value creation possible.

Flat systems cannot recognize enabling contributions. If rewards are based on "who made the trade," Carol gets everything. If rewards are based on "who provided liquidity," Bob gets everything. Alice, who created the pool, gets nothing.

### 12.2 How Shapley Recognizes Enabling Contributions

Shapley values naturally recognize enabling contributions because they consider *every possible ordering* of players joining the coalition.

In some orderings, Alice joins first and her marginal contribution is $0 (the pool alone generates no fees). In other orderings, Alice joins after Bob has already provided liquidity, and her contribution is still $0 (Bob cannot trade against himself). But in orderings where Alice and Bob have both joined and Carol joins last, Carol's marginal contribution is $100 (she generates the fees).

However, when Alice joins a coalition that already includes Carol but not Bob, Alice's marginal contribution is $0 (there is no liquidity to trade against). When Alice joins a coalition that includes Bob but not Carol, Alice's marginal contribution is $0 (there are no traders). Only when all three are present does the full $100 materialize.

The Shapley computation captures this: Alice and Bob are both necessary for Carol's trade to generate value. Their enabling contributions are reflected in their Shapley values, even though neither of them directly executed the trade.

### 12.3 Implications for Network Growth

Enabling contributions are especially important for early participants. The first liquidity provider, the first pool creator, the first developer who deployed the protocol -- these participants enabled all subsequent value creation. Shapley values ensure they are recognized:

- **Earlier participants who enabled later value are still recognized** through their marginal contribution to coalitions that include later participants.
- **But they are not overcompensated**, because their marginal contribution is computed precisely, not assumed.
- **And they cannot extract from later participants**, because the efficiency axiom bounds total distribution to total value created.

## 13. Incentive Properties

### 13.1 Proven Properties

The Shapley reward system produces the following incentive properties, each derived from the four axioms:

| Property | Derivation | Implication |
|---|---|---|
| **Incentives are aligned, not adversarial** | Symmetry + Efficiency | Participants benefit from others' success (larger v(N) means more to distribute) |
| **Early participants rewarded without extracting from late** | Null Player + Efficiency | Early rewards come from early value, not from later participants' deposits |
| **Late participants NOT penalized for joining late** | Symmetry + Null Player | Late joiners are evaluated on their own marginal contribution, not their position in a timeline |
| **Growth is cooperative** | Efficiency | Adding a productive participant increases v(N), benefiting everyone |
| **MLM dynamics mathematically constrained** | Efficiency + Event Isolation | Total rewards = total value, no compounding across events |
| **Rewards are symmetric and bounded** | Symmetry + Efficiency | Equal contribution = equal reward, total ≤ value created |

### 13.2 Game-Theoretic Equilibrium

Under the Shapley reward system, cooperation is the Nash equilibrium:

- **Cooperating** (contributing genuinely to events) yields positive Shapley value proportional to contribution.
- **Defecting** (attempting to extract without contributing) yields zero (null player axiom) or negative outcomes (reputation loss, slashing).
- **Free-riding** (participating without contributing) yields zero (null player axiom) but costs nothing -- so free-riders are tolerated but not rewarded.

The dominant strategy for any rational participant is: contribute genuinely to events where your marginal contribution is highest. This is precisely the behavior that maximizes network value.

### 13.3 Cooperation Without Morality

The system does not require participants to be altruistic, community-minded, or philosophically aligned with the protocol's values. It only requires them to be *rational*. A purely self-interested participant who maximizes their own Shapley value will, as a mathematical consequence, maximize their marginal contribution to the network.

This is the core insight: cooperation does not need to be moral. It needs to be profitable.

## 14. Computational Feasibility

### 14.1 The Complexity Problem

Exact Shapley value computation requires evaluating all 2^n coalitions, making it O(2^n) in the number of players. For a game with 20 players, this means evaluating over 1 million coalitions. For 30 players, over 1 billion.

### 14.2 Why Event-Based Games Solve This

Event-based games (Section 7) keep coalition sizes small. A typical referral event has 2-5 participants. A liquidity provision event has 3-10 participants. At these sizes, exact Shapley computation is trivial:

| Coalition Size | Coalitions to Evaluate | Time at 10^6 evaluations/sec |
|---|---|---|
| 3 | 8 | < 1 microsecond |
| 5 | 32 | < 1 microsecond |
| 10 | 1,024 | ~ 1 millisecond |
| 15 | 32,768 | ~ 33 milliseconds |
| 20 | 1,048,576 | ~ 1 second |

For the rare events with larger coalitions (e.g., a governance vote with 50 participants), Monte Carlo approximation provides provably accurate estimates:

```
Algorithm: Monte Carlo Shapley Approximation
Input: Game (N, v), number of samples M

For m = 1 to M:
    Generate random ordering π of N
    For each player i in π:
        S = {players before i in π}
        marginal_i += v(S ∪ {i}) - v(S)

φ_i ≈ marginal_i / M
```

With M = 10,000 samples, the approximation error is typically < 1% of the exact value, and computation takes milliseconds even for large games.

### 14.3 On-Chain vs. Off-Chain Computation

| Component | Location | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **Event detection** | On-chain | Events are defined by on-chain transactions (trades, referrals, LP actions) |
| **Characteristic function** | On-chain | v(S) is computed from on-chain data (fees generated, liquidity provided) |
| **Shapley computation** | Off-chain with on-chain verification | Computation is done off-chain for gas efficiency; results are submitted on-chain with Merkle proofs |
| **Distribution** | On-chain | Rewards are distributed by the ShapleyDistributor contract |
| **Quality weights** | On-chain | Computed and stored on-chain at epoch boundaries |

## 16. Conclusion

The Shapley reward system addresses the fundamental problem of value distribution in decentralized networks by replacing proxies for contribution with *actual marginal contribution*, computed through cooperative game theory.

The system's mathematical properties -- efficiency, symmetry, linearity, and the null player axiom -- are not aspirational goals. They are guaranteed outcomes of the Shapley computation. Rewards cannot exceed value created. Equal contributors receive equal rewards. Non-contributors receive nothing. These properties hold regardless of the participants' intentions, strategies, or moral dispositions.

The result is a reward system that is:

- **Fair by construction**: The four axioms guarantee fairness without requiring trust in any administrator.
- **Sustainable by construction**: The efficiency axiom bounds rewards to revenue, preventing inflation and Ponzi dynamics.
- **Anti-MLM by construction**: Event isolation and the efficiency axiom prevent compounding obligations.
- **Cooperative by construction**: The dominant strategy is genuine contribution, because that is what maximizes Shapley value.

Traditional reward systems ask: "How do we incentivize the behavior we want?" The Shapley system asks a different question: "What is the mathematically unique fair distribution?" The answer to the second question turns out to also answer the first, because fair distribution *is* the incentive. When participants know they will be rewarded in exact proportion to their actual contribution, the rational strategy is to contribute as much as possible.

> "The system naturally converges toward fair outcomes because unfair distributions are structurally impossible."
